Global electricity demand rose by 4.3\% in 2024, roughly 70\% higher than the 2.5\% increase in 2023, and is forecast to continue near 4\% annually through 2027~\cite{iea_electricity}.
A significant contributor to this demand is the rapid expansion of data centers, which currently consume 1.5\% of global electricity and are projected to grow by 15\% annually through 2030, a rate four times greater than that of any other sector \cite{iea_demand_report}.
This growth has historically been driven by the migration of on-premise deployments to the cloud, motivated by lower operational and management costs. More recently, the rise of Artificial Intelligence (AI) has accelerated demand further, with model training and inference consuming a large share of data center capacity.
The adoption of EVs is also an important reason for energy demand growth, with consumption tripling from 2024 through 2030, representing 2.5\% of global electricity demand \cite{iea_ev_report}.
Data centers account for 0.5\% of global energy-related CO\textsubscript{2} emissions today, and they are one of the few sectors whose share is projected to grow, reaching up to 1.4\% by 2030~\cite{iea_demand_report}.
Together, these trends are placing unprecedented pressure on electricity grids, intensifying the need to address their environmental impact.

This motivates a shift in focus from energy efficiency to carbon efficiency: energy efficiency measures useful work per unit of energy consumed (e.g., Joules), whereas carbon efficiency measures useful work per unit of GHG emitted (e.g., kg of CO\textsubscript{2}e).
Critically, high energy efficiency does not imply high carbon efficiency.
A highly energy-efficient workload running on a carbon-intensive grid still produces substantial carbon emissions, because every unit of energy it consumes is generated with high emissions regardless of how efficiently it is used. Conversely, a highly energy-inefficient workload running on a zero-carbon grid emits nothing, since no carbon is released in generating its electricity in the first place.
Compounding this, energy efficiency gains in data centers have historically been offset by their own consequences: by making computation cheaper, they drive greater demand, higher total energy consumption, and ultimately greater GHG emissions. Two principal strategies exist: (i) \textit{temporal shifting} and (ii) \textit{spatial shifting}. Temporal shifting defers or interrupts a workload so it runs during periods of lower carbon intensity. Spatial shifting migrates a workload to a region with a cleaner grid.
The applicability of these strategies depends on the nature of the workload: a continuous workload must execute uninterrupted once started, while an interruptible workload can be paused and resumed without affecting its outcome.
For example, an online inference service is a continuous workload, while a checkpointed rendering job is an interruptible one.

This planning depends on carbon intensity forecasts, making forecasting a critical enabler of carbon-aware optimization \cite{grid_observability}.
Previous forecasting efforts have focused on maximizing accuracy by minimizing error metrics such as mean absolute percentage error (MAPE), building data-intensive and computationally expensive neural networks \cite{maji_carboncast_2022,yan_ensembleci_2025,zhang_gnn-based_2023}.
However, it has not been examined whether higher forecast accuracy actually produces better scheduling outcomes.

In this thesis, we show that what drives better carbon-aware optimizations is not accuracy alone. 
A forecast must also achieve high \textit{concordance}: formally, the concordance index of Harrell et al.~\cite{harrell_concordance_1996}, \red{is} defined as the probability that for any pair of observations, a model's predictions correctly preserve the ordering of the true values. 
In the context of carbon-aware optimizations, this means the forecast preserves the same ordering as the true carbon intensities, correctly identifying when and where grids will be cleanest and enabling carbon-aware optimizers to select those times and locations.
Carbon-aware optimizations that correctly identify the lowest-carbon times and locations will achieve better carbon outcomes, even when the predicted intensities themselves are imprecise.
To demonstrate this, we present \textit{LiteCast}, a lightweight carbon intensity forecasting model that prioritizes concordance, requires less historical data than state-of-the-art models, runs on basic compute hardware, and adapts quickly to changes in the electricity grid.
We evaluate LiteCast across 48 regions worldwide under a range of workloads (including synthetic batch jobs and a real supercomputer cluster trace), demonstrating near-optimal carbon scheduling performance.
