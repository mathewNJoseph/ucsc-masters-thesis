\subsection{How Carbon Intensity is Calculated}

The environmental impact of electricity consumption is quantified through carbon intensity, measured in grams of CO\textsubscript{2} equivalent per kilowatt-hour (g$\cdot$CO\textsubscript{2}eq/kWh).
This metric captures the amount of greenhouse gases (GHG) emitted per unit of electricity generated, accounting for multiple GHG through their CO\textsubscript{2} equivalent (CO\textsubscript{2}eq) warming potential \cite{ghg_protocol}.
Each energy source carries a distinct carbon factor, defined as the amount of CO\textsubscript{2}eq emitted per kWh of electricity it produces.
Carbon factors are calculated per energy source by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) and represent the direct emissions from the operational generation of electricity \cite{ipcc_carbon_factors_2014}.

The GHG Protocol categorizes emissions into three scopes based on their source \cite{ghg_protocol}.
\textit{Scope 1} covers direct emissions from sources owned or controlled by the reporting entity, such as on-site fuel combustion or company-operated vehicles.
\textit{Scope 2} covers indirect emissions from the generation of purchased electricity, heat, or steam consumed by the entity.
\textit{Scope 3} covers all other indirect emissions across the value chain, including embodied emissions from equipment manufacturing, transportation, and supply chain activities.
This thesis focuses on Scope 2 emissions, as carbon-aware scheduling targets the carbon intensity of the electricity that workloads consume.
Scope 1 emissions are constrained by fixed infrastructure decisions, while Scope 3 emissions are largely embodied in hardware that has already been manufactured and represent a sunk carbon cost that runtime scheduling cannot recover \cite{bashir_sunk_2024}.
Operational Scope 2 emissions are therefore the only component that scheduling decisions can meaningfully modulate.

Within Scope 2, two common signals are used to quantify the carbon intensity of grid electricity \cite{sukprasert_limitations_2024}.
The \textit{average carbon intensity} is the weighted average of the carbon factors of all generators currently supplying electricity to the grid, where each source is weighted by its share of total generation.
The \textit{marginal carbon intensity} represents the carbon factor of the specific generator that responds to the last incremental unit of demand.
Because not all generators respond equally to changes in demand, only the marginal generator is credited with the emissions associated with added consumption.
Although marginal intensity more precisely captures the impact of an additional unit of demand, it is highly volatile and difficult to observe in real time.
The average intensity signal, by contrast, is stable and directly observable from publicly reported generation data, and is the signal required for Scope 2 reporting under the GHG Protocol.
Consistent with prior work \cite{maji_carboncast_2022,yan_ensembleci_2025,zhang_gnn-based_2023}, this thesis focuses on average carbon intensity.

The average carbon intensity of a region at a given time is computed as:
\begin{equation}
    CI_t = \frac{\sum_{i} E_{i,t} \cdot CF_i}{\sum_{i} E_{i,t}}
\end{equation}
where $E_{i,t}$ is the energy generated by source $i$ at time $t$ (in kWh) and $CF_i$ is the carbon factor of source $i$ (in g$\cdot$CO\textsubscript{2}eq/kWh).
The resulting carbon intensity reflects the instantaneous emissions profile of the grid based on its current generation mix.

\subsection{Energy Source Mix}


An electricity grid draws from a portfolio of generation sources that differ in their carbon factors, dispatchability, and cost.
These sources fall into two broad categories: carbon-intensive fossil fuels and low-carbon sources, which include renewables and nuclear power.

The carbon emissions associated with an energy source can be measured along two timelines.
\textit{Operational emissions} are released during the generation of electricity itself, primarily from fuel combustion or process emissions at the plant.
\textit{Lifecycle emissions} include operational emissions together with those from upstream and downstream activities: raw material extraction, plant construction, fuel transport, and decommissioning or waste handling.
Every source carries both, and even sources with zero operational emissions accumulate non-trivial lifecycle emissions through these activities.
This thesis characterizes grid carbon intensity using operational emissions for two reasons: only operational emissions vary at the timescales runtime scheduling can act on, and lifecycle emissions are largely sunk costs already incurred when the plant was built.

Fossil fuels, primarily coal and natural gas, carry high carbon factors at every stage of their lifecycle.
Coal emits approximately 760~g$\cdot$CO\textsubscript{2}eq/kWh during operation, with mining and transport adding further to its lifecycle footprint; coal remains valued as a baseload source because its output is continuously available regardless of weather or demand.
Natural gas emits roughly 370~g$\cdot$CO\textsubscript{2}eq/kWh during operation and has a similarly extractive supply chain, but its prices and availability are tightly tied to global politics.
Despite their environmental cost, both are highly dispatchable: operators can increase or decrease their output on demand, making them reliable tools for balancing supply and demand.

Zero-carbon sources, including wind, solar, hydropower, and nuclear, emit no direct carbon during operation, but each carries lifecycle emissions from the manufacturing, construction, or extraction activities that precede generation.
Solar panels and wind turbines require energy-intensive manufacturing of silicon, rare-earth metals, and structural components~\cite{iea_critical_minerals_2021}, though their long operational lifespans typically amortize these emissions over time.
Wind and solar are also non-dispatchable: their output depends entirely on prevailing weather conditions and cannot be controlled by grid operators.
Hydropower is partially dispatchable through reservoir management but is subject to seasonal water availability, and constructing a dam requires flooding large natural habitats.
Nuclear power is insensitive to weather, but its dispatchability is limited: reactors can only ramp output over hours rather than minutes. \red{Son: Idk what this means.}
Its lifecycle emissions come from uranium mining and fuel processing, and the long-term handling of radioactive waste remains an unresolved challenge, as no permanent disposal repository is yet operational in the United States.
Nuclear capacity is also slow to expand: its share of U.S. electricity generation is projected to decline from 17\% in 2025 to roughly 12\% by 2050~\cite{eia_nuclear}, limiting its contribution to future grid decarbonization.

Each regional grid is managed by a balancing authority, which is responsible for continuously matching electricity supply with \red{Son: "to" not "with"} demand in real time.
The carbon intensity of a grid at any moment is determined by which generators the balancing authority has dispatched to meet current demand.
The composition of these portfolios varies dramatically across regions, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:bg-energy-source}.
Regions with a high share of renewables and nuclear in their generation mix, such as France (approximately 70\% nuclear) or Sweden (dominated by hydro and nuclear), have persistently low carbon intensity, while regions that rely heavily on coal and gas, such as parts of the United States Midwest or Poland, maintain high carbon intensity.

Carbon intensity is not static: it varies continuously as the generation mix shifts in response to demand, weather, and grid conditions.
This variability operates across two dimensions, temporal and spatial, and understanding both is essential for effective carbon-aware scheduling.

\textit{Temporal variability} arises from the interaction of demand patterns, local weather, and renewable generation cycles.
Electricity demand follows predictable diurnal rhythms tied to human behavior: demand is lowest overnight, rises sharply in the morning as households and businesses come online, and peaks in the early evening when both commercial and residential activity are at their highest.
During periods of high demand, grid operators dispatch additional generators to maintain supply, often relying on carbon-intensive peaking units such as natural gas turbines, which temporarily elevates carbon intensity.
Renewable generation adds a second layer of temporal variability.
Solar output follows a predictable daily cycle, peaking around midday and dropping to zero at night, though unexpected weather such as cloud cover can introduce variability and intermittency.
Wind generation is less predictable, varying over hours and days depending on local and regional weather patterns.
Figure~\ref{fig:bg-energy-variance} illustrates these dynamics in California: solar generation traces a clean daily arc, wind generation fluctuates irregularly across hours, and demand follows its own diurnal rhythm that does not align with either source.
The resulting interaction is visible in the same figure as a pronounced mid-day dip in carbon intensity, when solar displaces fossil fuel generation, followed by a rise in the evening as solar output falls and demand remains high.
Seasonal effects compound these daily cycles: sunshine is lower in winter, heating demand rises in cold months, and wind patterns shift between seasons, each affecting the generation mix and the resulting carbon intensity.
Weather also affects demand directly: temperature extremes drive energy consumption for heating and cooling, which is frequently met by carbon-intensive peaking units, raising carbon intensity during heat waves and cold snaps.

\textit{Spatial variability} reflects the fundamental differences in generation infrastructure across regions.
Figure~\ref{fig:bg-spatial-ci} shows the distribution of hourly carbon intensity across a representative set of regional grids worldwide, sorted from cleanest to most carbon-intensive.
Carbon intensity varies by roughly 25$\times$ between the least and most  carbon-intensive grids: France's nuclear-heavy grid maintains an average carbon intensity near 25~g$\cdot$CO\textsubscript{2}eq/kWh, while coal-dependent regions such as Poland or the Seychelles average above 600~g$\cdot$CO\textsubscript{2}eq/kWh.
Even within a single country, carbon intensity varies significantly between regions: California's grid, with a high share of solar and wind, is substantially cleaner than the coal-heavy Midwest.

\subsection{Intermittency of Renewable Energy}

Wind and solar energy are inherently intermittent: they generate electricity only when weather conditions permit, and how much they output cannot be controlled by grid operators, except when supply exceeds demand and the excess output is shed through curtailment.
Unlike coal or gas plants, which can be ramped up or shut down on demand, renewable generators cannot be ramped up at will: operators can only reduce output below what the weather allows, never increase it.
A sudden drop in wind speed or an overcast sky can cause renewable output to fall within minutes, forcing the grid to compensate with fast-ramping fossil fuel generators.
This asymmetry, where output can be capped but not raised, is a fundamental physical constraint of renewable generation.

As electricity grids integrate higher shares of renewables to reduce their carbon footprint, this intermittency becomes an increasingly important source of carbon intensity variability.
Grids with high renewable penetration, such as Denmark or Texas, exhibit large and rapid swings in carbon intensity that are difficult to predict, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:bg-temporal-ci}.
The variability in these regions is substantially higher than in grids anchored by stable baseload sources such as nuclear or hydro.

This layered uncertainty, from weather conditions to renewable output to carbon intensity, makes precise long-horizon carbon intensity forecasting inherently difficult.
A forecaster must anticipate not only how demand will evolve but also how wind speeds and solar irradiance will change, and how the grid will respond to those conditions.
Compounding this challenge, renewable capacity is expanding rapidly: in the United States, the EIA projects 43.4~GW of new utility-scale solar capacity in 2026 (a 60\% increase over 2025) and 11.8~GW of new wind capacity (more than double the prior year), with renewables and battery storage together accounting for nearly 80\% of new generating capacity \cite{eia_capacity_2026}.
As new generators come online, the underlying generation mix shifts continuously, meaning forecasting models trained on long historical windows risk becoming stale as the grid they were trained on no longer reflects the grid that exists today.
This motivates the need for forecasting approaches that are adaptive to changing grid conditions rather than reliant solely on historical patterns learned from large datasets.

\subsection{Carbon-Aware Optimization Strategies}

Carbon-aware optimizers exploit the temporal and spatial variability of carbon intensity to reduce the operational carbon footprint of flexible workloads.
Two principal strategies are used, corresponding to the two dimensions of load flexibility.

\textit{Temporal shifting} defers or interrupts a workload to align its execution with periods of lower carbon intensity within a deadline window.
For a continuous workload, this means delaying the start time until a low-carbon period is forecast to begin.
For an interruptible workload, the scheduler selects the lowest-carbon hours within the deadline window and distributes execution across them.
The amount of carbon savings achievable through temporal shifting depends on the magnitude of temporal variability in the local grid and the amount of slack available in the workload's deadline \cite{sukprasert_limitations_2024}.
Google's carbon-intelligent computing platform applies temporal shifting to defer batch datacenter workloads to hours when renewable energy supply is highest \cite{google_data_center_work_harder}.
Wiesner et al.\ demonstrate how temporal shifting can reduce cloud carbon emissions across different European regions \cite{wiesner_lets_2021}.

\textit{Spatial shifting} migrates a workload to a geographic region with lower current carbon intensity.
This strategy is applicable to workloads with spatial flexibility, such as distributed cloud services.
CASPER \cite{souza_casper_2023} formulates spatial shifting for distributed web services as a multi-objective optimization problem balancing carbon footprint and latency constraints, achieving up to 70\% carbon reduction.
Caribou \cite{gsteiger_caribou_2024} applies fine-grained geospatial shifting to serverless functions, enabling sub-request-level migration to cleaner regions.

The two strategies are complementary and can be combined for workloads that possess both temporal and spatial flexibility.
The degree of achievable savings depends on the variability of carbon intensity across time and space, as well as the flexibility of the workload.
Sukprasert et al.\ provide a comprehensive analysis of the practical upper bounds of both strategies across 123 cloud regions, showing that while carbon savings are meaningful, they are bounded and diminish as grids become cleaner \cite{sukprasert_limitations_2024}.

\subsection{Carbon Forecasting for Scheduling}

Carbon-aware optimization requires planning ahead.
To schedule a workload at the optimal time or location, an optimizer must know when and where carbon intensity will be lowest before the workload begins.
This planning requires forecasts: without a look-ahead window of future carbon intensity, a scheduler cannot identify the best execution window and must resort to reacting to current conditions rather than anticipating future ones \cite{grid_observability}.
Carbon intensity forecasting is therefore a critical enabler of carbon-aware optimization.

Effective forecasting must provide predictions over the full \textit{window length} of the job, defined as the interval within which the job must both start and finish.
Let $\mathcal{J}$ denote a job submitted to the scheduler.
The window length $\mathcal{J}_{\text{window}}$ is the sum of the job's intrinsic runtime $\mathcal{J}_{\text{length}}$ and the slack $\mathcal{J}_{\text{slack}}$ the user is willing to tolerate before completion:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{J}_{\text{window}} = \mathcal{J}_{\text{length}} + \mathcal{J}_{\text{slack}}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{J}_{\text{length}}$ is the time required to execute the job and $\mathcal{J}_{\text{slack}}$ is the additional flexibility the user grants the scheduler to defer execution.
Window lengths can range from a few hours for short batch jobs to multiple days for long-running AI training workloads with large deadline slack.
